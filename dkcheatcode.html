The Dunning-Kruger Curve

The Dunning-Kruger effect describes how confidence often peaks before competence. This visual helps frame our prompt categories and where they apply in the learning journey.

The Dunning-Kruger effect is a cognitive bias where people with limited knowledge or skill in a particular area greatly overestimate their own competence. Named after psychologists David Dunning and Justin Kruger, this effect creates a dangerous confidence curve: the less you know, the more confident you feel about what you think you know.

  • Mount Stupid: High confidence, little understanding
  • Valley of Despair: Confidence drops as complexity becomes visible
  • Slope of Enlightenment: True learning and reliable skill begin to grow
  • Plateau of Sustainability: Quiet competence, confidence aligned with ability

Understanding the Dunning-Kruger effect helps us tailor learning support based on where someone is on the confidence curve. Early learners may feel overly confident, so we gently expose gaps in their understanding to spark curiosity. As they hit the “Valley of Despair” and realize the complexity, we offer reassurance, structure, and small wins to keep them going. As their competence grows, we challenge them with real-world tasks and critical reflection. In short, we match our teaching to their mindset—balancing encouragement with challenge to help confidence and competence grow together. Ask ChatGPT

Mount Stupidity

High confidence, low competence. Use these prompts to reveal shallow understanding.

Outline Expander

Category: Breadth

Reveals whether someone understands the full landscape of a topic.

Use Cases: Assess general awareness, scoping exercises
Act as an outline expander. List the major components of [topic]
What are the key disciplines involved in [topic]?
Expand on topic #.

Explainer Ladder

Category: Transferability

Tests if someone can explain the topic at different levels of abstraction.

Use Cases: Teaching, stakeholder alignment
Explain [concept] to a 10-year-old, then to a CEO, then to an expert.
Summarize [topic] in 3 versions: one for a beginner, one for an intermediate learner, and one for a technical peer.
Break down [problem] as if you were talking to a confused customer, then as if you were talking to an engineering team.”

Valley of Despair

Awareness of complexity sets in. Use these prompts to build depth and self-awareness.

Edge Case Trigger

Category: Depth

Tests understanding of exceptions and nuance in a system.

Use Cases: Stress-testing, model evaluation
What are edge cases or failure scenarios in [topic]?
Where does this approach usually break down?

Past Experience Filter

Category: Lived Knowledge

Checks for real-world exposure and lessons learned.

Use Cases: Hiring, retrospectives, mentoring
What did you get wrong when you first worked on [topic]?
Tell me about a time your approach didn’t work—and why.

Slope of Enlightenment

Skill grows. Confidence returns with perspective. Use these prompts to build judgment and reliability.

Trade-off Navigator

Category: Judgment

Encourages critical thinking about competing options.

Use Cases: Architecture choices, project debates
What are the trade-offs between [Option A] and [Option B]?
When is [Method A] better than [Method B], and when is it not?

Self-Critique Request

Category: Reflection

Tests for self-awareness and the ability to spot flaws or biases.

Use Cases: Prompt tuning, performance reviews
What would a skeptic say about this response?
Critique this answer from the perspective of an expert.
If a domain expert reviewed this, where would they likely challenge or improve it?
What are three ways this explanation might be wrong or misleading?
